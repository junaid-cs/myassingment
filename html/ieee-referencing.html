What Is The IEEE Referencing Style?

The IEEE referencing style follows the numbered form of referencing. It was developed by the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) to use for citing references in electrical, electronic, and computing publications.

This referencing style comprises two components:

  • In-text citations– You have to number the sources in square brackets, e.g. [1], in the order of their appearance in the paper.
  • Reference list– You must include this section at the end of the paper and number each reference in the same order as the in-text citations.

IEEE In-Text Citation Rules With Examples

Since the IEEE referencing style follows the numbered form of reference, you must include a number within square brackets whenever you cite a source. While our IEEE citation machine can help you compose in-text citations for your assignments, you should always keep the following points in mind:

  • No need to provide new numbers for repeated references

These in-text citation numbers appear in the order of their appearance in the paper. However, if you’re citing the same source again later, you don’t have to assign a new number for it. For example:

"Hosier, in his new theory [1]…."

"The theory by Hosier [1]…."

  • If you're citing several sources, separate each number with a comma

Sometimes, you might have to include multiple citations for a particular section. In this case, you can separate them by a comma. For example,

" According to several theories, [1], [3], [7] the results point towards …."

  • Author-prominent and information-prominent in-text citation

When using IEEE referencing at Monash University, you’ll notice that the placement of your in-text citation depends on whether your sentence is author-prominent or information-prominent. For example,

Author-prominent: "Matthew and Clive [4] indicate the results…."

Information-prominent: "These results [5] are vital to the…."
